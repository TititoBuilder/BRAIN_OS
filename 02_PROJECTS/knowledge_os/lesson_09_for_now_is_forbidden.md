Here's what you need to know to close this course — one phrase you have banned from your own work, and why banning it ties together everything you have just heard. The phrase is for now. As in, I will just do it this way for now. For now is forbidden, because for now is how temporary becomes permanent without anyone deciding it should.

Walk back through what you have learned and notice that nearly every problem started as a for-now. The shadow setting that overrode your repository was a for-now — a quick fix that solved something urgent once and then stayed, silently undermining every future change. The hand-maintained list that drifted was a for-now — easier to type out than to derive, until it started listing files that no longer existed. The wrong audio came from a script that grabbed a filename instead of an ID, almost certainly because a filename was easier in the moment. Each of these worked, for now. And each of them turned into debt that cost you far more later than doing it properly would have cost up front.

That is the trap of for now. It is not that the shortcut is wrong in the moment — it usually works in the moment, which is exactly why it is dangerous. The cost is deferred. It shows up weeks later as a silent wrong answer, a drift between two copies, a bug invisible from the outside. By then you have forgotten the shortcut was ever temporary. It is just how the system works now, quietly broken, and you are debugging a symptom far from the cause.

So what does forbidding for now actually mean in practice? It does not mean everything must be perfect or finished. It means you do not build something you already know is wrong and tell yourself you will fix it later. When you see the right way and the quick way, and you choose the quick way knowingly, you have just created debt with your eyes open. The discipline is to either do it right, or — if you genuinely cannot right now — to make the shortcut loud. Write down that it is temporary. Leave the signal that says this is not finished. What you must not do is let a known-wrong thing disappear into the system looking permanent.

And here is how every lesson in this course folds into this one. Verify against reality, because for-now beliefs are the ones you never checked. Hold the four layers, because shortcuts hide by blurring which layer you are really touching. One source of truth, because a second source is a for-now copy that drifts. Derive instead of duplicate, for the same reason. Keep your keys disciplined, because a sloppy name is a for-now that breaks the join. Decide how things fail at the boundaries, because silent failure is a for-now you never see. Ask the source if it changed, because assuming is the laziest for-now of all. Every lesson is a specific defense against the same enemy — the convenient shortcut that becomes permanent because no one decided it should.

Your real constraint was never understanding. It was time. And for now is what time pressure whispers to you. The whole point of building systems that make dividends is that the work you do properly once keeps paying you, while the work you shortcut keeps charging you. So when you hear yourself say for now, stop. That is the sound of debt being created. Decide, on purpose, whether you are doing this right — or at least making the shortcut impossible to forget. That single habit, more than any tool, is what keeps a system you can trust.
