Here's what you need to know before anything else about your system — one rule that sits underneath every other lesson in this course. Confident text is not the truth. A claim is not a fact until you have checked it against what is actually there.

That sounds obvious. It isn't, because almost every problem you have ever hit came from the gap between what something claimed and what was true. A script's comment said it did one thing while the code did another. A note in your head said the index was fine when it wasn't. A tool reported everything was healthy while quietly measuring nothing. Each time, the trap was the same — you trusted the description instead of inspecting the reality.

So the rule is simple to say and hard to live. Before you change something, look at the thing itself. Not the documentation about it. Not your memory of it. Not what a confident voice — including your own, including mine — tells you it does. The actual file, the actual output, the actual state on disk. You read the code line by line. You run the command and look at what comes back. You open the graph and see whether the connections are really there.

Why does this matter so much for you specifically? Because your constraint is time, not understanding. You move fast, you build a lot, and the temptation under time pressure is to act on what you assume is true and keep moving. That assumption is exactly where the debt hides. A wrong belief held confidently costs you far more later than a thirty-second check costs you now. The check is cheap. The surprise is expensive.

And here is the part that makes this a discipline rather than a slogan. It applies even when you are sure. Especially when you are sure. The moments you got burned were not the moments you felt uncertain and double-checked. They were the moments you felt certain and skipped the look. Certainty is not evidence. It is a feeling, and feelings about code are not facts about code.

Every lesson after this one is really the same lesson wearing different clothes. When you hear about the wrong audio that took weeks to trace, that is this rule. When you hear about the documentation that lied, this rule. When you hear why you delete a shadow setting instead of trusting it, this rule again. They are all instances of one move — go look, do not assume.

So carry this as the frame. When something feels settled, ask one question. Have I actually checked, or do I just believe it? If you have not checked, you do not know. You only think you do. Go look.
